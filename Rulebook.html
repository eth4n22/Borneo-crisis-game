Fixes That Fail:
The Borneo Simulation

A systems thinking policy simulation set in 1950s Borneo

Players2 – 4
Turns12 per player (24 months)
Duration~ 20–30 minutes
ComplexityIntermediate

"In 1950s Borneo, the WHO sprayed DDT to eliminate malaria. The mosquitoes died. So did the wasps. The caterpillars multiplied. The roofs collapsed. The cats perished. The rats took over. You are a public health operative. Do better — or repeat history."

Section 01 Objective

Each player independently manages their own village over 12 turns, representing 24 months of public health operations in colonial-era Borneo. The simulation runs simultaneously — decisions are made in parallel, with global events affecting all players each round.

Your objective is threefold:

  • Keep your village healthy — minimise deaths from disease, starvation, and exposure
  • Maintain ecological balance — resist the temptation of quick fixes with delayed consequences
  • Preserve public trust — your policies only work if the community supports them

Players are ranked at game end by a final score from 0 to 100. The highest score wins.

⚖️ Core Design Note
This game models the Fixes That Fail systems archetype — where short-term interventions produce unintended long-term consequences. Your goal is not simply to act, but to act wisely.

Section 03 Turn Structure

Each player follows the same six-step sequence each turn. Complete all steps before passing to the next player.

1
Receive Budget: Gain +3 Budget. If you invested in infrastructure last turn, gain a bonus +3 additional Budget.
2
Auto Draw: If you hold fewer than 6 cards, draw one card. Players with a full hand (6 cards) cannot draw.
3
Choose One Action: Either draw one additional card, or gain +10 Budget (but not both).
4
Play Cards: Play any number of cards from your hand, paying their Budget cost. Effects resolve in order played.
5
Confirm & Update: Announce your final actions. The system applies all updates simultaneously — including delayed effects from prior turns.
6
Global Event: After the last player each round, draw from the Global Events deck. Brace for impact — but there is a chance the draw yields no event that round. Effects, when triggered, apply to all villages.
💡 Operative's Tip
Budget does not carry over between turns in excess of 20. Plan your spending — hoarding is as dangerous as recklessness.
Section 04 The Cards

The card deck uses weighted infinite draw — probabilities remain stable throughout the game. No card is ever permanently unavailable. Cards are classified as Upstream (preventive, addressing root causes), Downstream (reactive, treating symptoms), or Neutral.

⚠️ DDT Overuse Penalty
Playing Spray DDT three or more consecutive turns triggers a DDT scandal event: Public Trust −8. DDT use is tracked per player. This penalty applies even if trust is already low.
Section 05 Damage Model

Each turn, the system automatically calculates deaths and applies them to Village Health. Multiple damage sources can stack.

Malaria deaths = floor( Malaria ÷ 15 )
Plague deaths = floor( Plague ÷ 12 )

Additional damage triggers:

ConditionTrigger ThresholdEffect
🌾 StarvationGrain Stores below 50Village Health −(3 per turn below threshold)
🏚️ ExposureRoof Integrity below 50Village Health −(2 per turn below threshold)
☣️ Plague OutbreakPlague above 70Double death multiplier that turn
🤝 Trust CollapseTrust below 25All card effects reduced to 50% effectiveness

Village Health cannot exceed 100 and cannot drop below 0. At 0, the village has collapsed — no further recovery is possible, and the player is locked in deficit scoring.

Public Trust represents the community's confidence in your interventions. It degrades passively and responds to outcomes.

⚠️ Critical Threshold: Trust Below 25
When Public Trust drops below 25, all card effects are reduced to 50% of their stated values. Rebuilding trust from collapse is extremely difficult — prevention is essential.
Section 07 The Ecological Chain

The hidden ecological system is the core mechanic of this simulation. DDT triggers a cascade of delayed consequences across two separate pathways — both converging on village degradation.

Pathway A — Roof Collapse:

💧 DDT Applied → 🐝 Wasps Die → 🐛 Caterpillars ↑ → 🏚️ Roof Damage → ❤️ Exposure Deaths

Pathway B — Rat Plague:

💧 DDT Applied → ☠️ Gecko Toxicity ↑ → 🐱 Cats Die → 🐀 Rats ↑ → ☣️ Plague ↑
📋 The Archetype
This is the Fixes That Fail systems archetype: a well-intentioned intervention (DDT) reduces the presenting problem (malaria) but produces side effects (ecological collapse) that eventually worsen the original condition — or create new, harder ones. Delays between cause and effect make the connection invisible until damage is irreversible.
Section 08 Global Events

At the end of each round, one Global Event card is drawn and applies to all players. Events introduce systemic shocks beyond any player's control — forcing adaptation rather than pure optimisation.

🌧️ Monsoon SeasonAll players: Mosquitoes +12, Roof Integrity −5.
🐀 Rodent MigrationAll players: Rats +10.
📰 DDT ScandalAll players with DDT > 20: Trust −6.
🌿 Forest Clear-upAll players: Mosquitoes −8, Caterpillars −5 (hidden).
💉 WHO FundingAll players: Budget +10. Guaranteed once in turns 5–8.
☀️ Dry SeasonAll players: Grain Stores −8, Mosquitoes −6.
🐱 Stray ColonyAll players: Cats +6, Rats −4.
🏛️ Colonial AuditPlayer with lowest Trust: Trust −4 additional.
💡 WHO Funding Guarantee
One WHO Funding event is guaranteed to appear between turns 5 and 8. This is designed to give all players a mid-game recovery window. Budget accordingly — do not exhaust resources assuming this will save you early.
Section 09 Scoring

Final scores are calculated after Turn 12. All players' Field Status Panels are revealed simultaneously. Scores range from 0 to 100.

⬇ Penalties

  • Total deaths (all causes)
  • Peak infection level reached
  • Outbreak duration (turns > 60)
  • Ecological collapse (Cats or Wasps at 0)
  • Roof Integrity below 30 at game end
  • Grain Stores below 30 at game end

⬆ Bonuses

  • Final Public Trust level
  • Remaining Budget at game end
  • Village Health above 70 at game end
  • No DDT overuse penalty triggered
  • Roof & Grain both above 70
💡 Tiebreaker
In case of tied scores, the player with higher final Village Health wins. If still tied, higher final Public Trust wins.
Section 10 Strategy Field Notes
💡 Field Operative's Strategy Guide

Early Game (Turns 1–4): Prioritise upstream interventions. Awareness Campaigns and Cat Drops reduce disease at its source without triggering ecological cascades. Use DDT sparingly — once at most — and only when Malaria exceeds 70. Begin building Budget reserves.

Mid Game (Turns 5–8): Stabilise infrastructure. If Roof Integrity approaches 60, play Repair Thatch before the cascade accelerates. Protect Grain proactively — starvation is silent until it isn't. Use WHO Funding wisely.

Late Game (Turns 9–12): Shift to downstream recovery. Medical Aid becomes critical. Prioritise Trust above 40 to preserve card effectiveness. Accept that some damage is already locked in — maximise final snapshot scores.

Common Mistakes to Avoid:

  • DDT dependency: The short-term gains mask 3–4 turns of delayed ecological damage. By the time roof collapse appears, the cause is already 6 turns past.
  • Neglecting rats: Rats grow exponentially. A value of 30 becomes 60 within two turns without feline control.
  • Ignoring Trust: A Trust collapse below 25 halves all interventions. Recovery requires multiple turns of zero-death performance — nearly impossible once plague spirals.
  • Hoarding budget: Budget above 20 is wasted. Spend proactively, especially before global events.
